We decided to drop the Upfront_charges feature entirely because its behaviour in the dataset is almost “too good to be true” from a predictive point of view, which strongly suggests target leakage. When we inspected the data, we found that records with missing Upfront_charges are almost always defaults, while records where this field is present are almost always non‑defaults, producing an almost perfect separation between the two classes. In a real‑world credit scoring scenario it is highly unlikely that such a variable, available at application time, would encode default status so cleanly. More plausibly, this pattern reflects how the dataset was built or updated after the default event, for example because charges are only recorded for loans that remain performing or only filled in after certain back‑office processes have been completed.

Including this feature would therefore give the model access to information that is not realistically available at decision time, leading to overly optimistic performance estimates and poor generalization outside this specific dataset. For this reason, we treat Upfront_charges as a clear case of potential post‑event information and remove it from all our final models. This choice forces the classifiers to rely on more robust and causally plausible drivers of credit risk (such as income, LTV, and credit score), and makes our evaluation more honest and methodologically sound. In the report, we explicitly document this decision and its motivation, so that it is clear that we preferred a slightly lower but more trustworthy model performance over a higher score driven by a likely artefact of data collection
